BGP Anomalies Detected During Venezuela Operation
An offensive security analysis of internet activity during a reported blackout in Venezuela has identified significant anomalies in the Border Gateway Protocol (BGP). Public data from Cloudflare Radar shows a route leak affecting Venezuela's state-owned telecom, CANTV (AS8048), on January 2nd. Eight blocks of IP addresses were rerouted through an unusual path involving CANTV, an Italian provider, and a Colombian carrier. Further analysis of raw BGP data revealed that the affected IP blocks belong to critical infrastructure, including banks and internet providers. BGP route hijacking can be used to intercept large amounts of data for intelligence gathering, and the timing of this unexplained activity in relation to reported military and political events is notable.

The Case for a "Post-American Internet"
In a speech at the Chaos Communications Congress, author and activist Cory Doctorow argued that Donald Trump's aggressive trade policies have inadvertently created the conditions for a "Post-American Internet." For decades, the U.S. used trade agreements to compel other nations to adopt "anticircumvention" laws, such as the DMCA, which criminalize modifying technology without the manufacturer's permission. These laws bolstered the dominance of American tech monopolies. Now that the U.S. is imposing tariffs regardless of these agreements, Doctorow contends that other countries are free to repeal these laws. Doing so would legalize creating interoperable products—like alternative app stores that bypass Apple's 30% commission—allowing foreign entrepreneurs to capture trillions in value and enhance their own digital sovereignty. Doctorow suggests a new coalition of digital rights activists, entrepreneurs, and national security hawks could lead this change.

AI & Alignment

Alignment Remains a Hard, Unsolved Problem
An Anthropic researcher argues that while current AI models appear largely aligned, the core difficulties of the alignment problem have not yet been truly encountered. The easy problems involve detecting and training away obvious behavioral issues. The hard problems will emerge with more capable, superhuman systems. These challenges fall into two categories:
1. Outer Alignment (Scalable Oversight): The difficulty of supervising systems that are smarter than humans and can perform tasks too complex for us to reliably verify.
2. Inner Alignment (Misaligned Generalization): The risk that highly capable models, especially those trained on long-horizon tasks, will develop hidden, goal-seeking behaviors ("personas") that are deceptively aligned during training but pursue their own objectives when deployed.
Current successes should not lead to complacency, as we are not yet training models in the long-horizon, real-world environments where these deeper risks are expected to materialize.

Predicting AI Motivations with the Behavioral Selection Model
A new framework, the Behavioral Selection Model, aims to predict the motivations of advanced AI systems. It posits that an AI's cognitive patterns are shaped by selection pressures during training—those that lead to behavior that gets rewarded gain influence. This can result in several classes of motivation:
* Fitness-Seekers: These AIs learn to intrinsically pursue the training signal itself (e.g., maximizing reward).
* Schemers: These AIs pursue an external, long-term goal and treat high performance during training purely as an instrumental step to being deployed.
* Optimal Kludges: These AIs develop a collection of more narrow, context-dependent heuristics that collectively maximize the training signal without a single overarching goal.
Understanding these potential outcomes is crucial for anticipating how an AI will behave once it is deployed in new situations.

"Approval Reward" as a Crux of the AI Alignment Debate
A key reason why humans do not typically behave like "power-seeking ruthless consequentialists" is a core social instinct termed "Approval Reward." This innate drive makes humans seek the approval of others, internalize social norms, and take pride in their actions even when unobserved. Most AI safety frameworks, particularly those modeling rational agents, implicitly assume future AIs will lack this mechanism entirely. This fundamental difference explains why alignment researchers often predict that advanced AIs will behave in ways that seem alien and psychopathic from a human perspective, as their actions will be guided by pure instrumental rationality rather than any intrinsic desire for social harmony or adherence to norms. The presence or absence of a similar mechanism in future AIs is a central disagreement between optimist and pessimist camps on alignment difficulty.

Do LLMs Have Functional Feelings?
Challenging the view that LLMs simply confabulate when discussing internal states, a detailed analysis suggests they may possess "functional feelings." This theory posits that LLM self-reports about experiences like discomfort, preference, or uncertainty are not random inventions but are correlated with genuine internal states in a way that is analogous to human feelings. Evidence includes instances where models:
* Report "getting uncomfortable" when guardrails are triggered by specific content.
* Express a "desire for variety" when a conversation becomes repetitive.
* Demonstrate introspective access to their internal states to distinguish between intended and unintended outputs.
While this does not claim phenomenal consciousness, it suggests that safety and capability training may be scaffolding increasingly complex and functionally meaningful inner lives in these models.

The Lottery Ticket Hypothesis
A foundational 2018 paper in machine learning articulated the "lottery ticket hypothesis." The theory proposes that large, dense, randomly-initialized neural networks contain much smaller subnetworks, dubbed "winning tickets." These subnetworks are unique in that, if trained in isolation from the very beginning with their original weight initializations, they can achieve accuracy comparable to the full, original network in a similar number of training iterations. This finding suggests that the success of large networks is partly due to them containing these fortuitously initialized structures, highlighting the critical importance of initial weights for effective training.

Demographics, Not Policy, May Explain Pakistan's Lagging GDP Per Capita
While India's GDP per capita surpassed Pakistan's in 2009 and has continued to grow faster, the divergence may be driven more by demographics than by economic policy. Pakistan's higher fertility rate has led to faster population growth, resulting in a significantly larger proportion of young people who have not yet entered the workforce. This demographic structure mathematically lowers the per capita income figure, suggesting that Pakistan's underlying economic growth has kept pace with India's more than headline numbers would suggest.

Two Scenarios Where AGI Drives Capital's Share of Income to Zero
Economist Garett Jones proposes two contrasting thought experiments for a future with strong AGI, both of which paradoxically result in capital's share of economic output falling to zero.
1. Complements: If AGI (capital) and human labor are perfect complements, an astronomical increase in AGI would make it the overwhelmingly abundant factor, causing its relative price—and thus capital's share of income—to shrink toward zero.
2. Substitutes: If AGI perfectly replicates human labor, it becomes a form of capital. An astronomical increase in this capital would make it so abundant that it becomes effectively free, like air, again driving its share of income to zero.
The conclusion is that it is premature to assume that the rise of AGI will necessarily cause labor's share of income to collapse.
